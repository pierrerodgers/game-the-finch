Paperboy Prince is a politician, artist, and rapper, and the founder of Bushwick’s Love Gallery. In 2021 Paperboy competed in New York City’s mayoral race on a platform of reparations, universal basic income and defunding the police, and has also staged food drives at the Love Gallery and released a concept mixtape about Andrew Yang.
Momo Hudes and Joanie Cappetta are the co-founders of Benny’s Club, a surfing collective for BIPOC and queer folk based in New York City. Benny’s Club was most recently covered by Vice, in addition to the Cut and other outlets; since 2020 Hudes and Cappetta have been hosting surfing lessons, meet-ups, and beach clean-ups in Far Rockaway, Queens.
Raf Khalilov and Jane Paknia composed Episode Seven’s original music, with Paknia responsible for the two synth-heavy pieces that score Lyla Trilling and Isabel Gilmour’s original sequence, as well as the central interview segment; and Khalilov behind the piano theme heard whenever the episode’s fate-conscious bums turn up. Paknia’s Spotify overflows with sonic novelties; you can find out about upcoming gigs on Instagram. So far, Khalilov’s published music is mostly exclusive to Sankyo Stream.
Ron Magliozzi is a film curator at the Museum of Modern Art, and has worked at the museum since 1979. “Private Lives Public Spaces,” the exhibition referenced in Episode Seven, screened hundreds of Super 8 reels excavated from MoMA’s archives, collected over decades and pored over by Magliozzi and the exhibition’s team. One of the most popular shows in which Magliozzi took part was MoMA’s 2009 Tim Burton retrospective.
Contrary to popular belief, Sankyo did not happen upon Jesse Newman and Justin Scheer in a random park in Bushwick—Newman is a high school friend and recent New York transplant—but the skaters proved natural screen presences. Newman has also recently forayed into modeling.
Lyla Trilling and Isabel Gilmour made their Sankyo Stream debut as writers with Episode Seven (Gilmour previously performed in Episode Three), but both are avid creators at Columbia University. They’ve each contributed to The Blue and White, Columbia’s beloved undergraduate magazine; Gilmour’s short story “Jean Luc and I” and Trilling’s profile of The Cut columnist Brock Colyar are recent highlights. Trilling also serves as one of the magazine’s senior editors.
Thomas Baker, Noah Chacon and Theo Elfaizy-Phillips are actors and students (Baker and Elfaizy Phillips at Columbia, Chacon at NYU). In December, Baker acted in Barnard Theatre’s beguiling departmental production of Orlando; Chacon stars in the 2021 short "Voices of God.”
